Mechanism E — Radiant Projection (Optical Imprint Hypothesis)

E.1 Concept & Rationale

Mechanism E tests whether a camera-obscura-type optical projection using Renaissance tools could yield a true-to-scale negative image on treated linen.
The premise: Leonardo’s known experiments with concave mirrors, burning mirrors, and “dark chambers” provided the technology to project a luminous subject—whether sculpture, bas-relief, or living sitter—onto a sensitised cloth.
This transforms the Shroud from “contact relic” into optical manuscript: a fusion of geometry, light, and time.


---

E.2 Historical Evidence

Codex Arundel 173r and Madrid I outline mirror geometries and lens curvature tables.

Leonardo describes “a window that brings the sun into darkness,” matching the camera obscura principle.

Workshop notes on varnished cloths and convex mirrors coincide with his anatomical studies (1490–1505)—exactly the Shroud’s dating window.

His use of heliographic tracing for perspective (De Luce e Ombra) mirrors this concept.

The image thus forms via radiant exposure, not contact, preserving proportion without distortion.

E.4 Materials & Setup

Chamber: wooden box or tent (1.5 m × 1 m × 1 m) lined matte black.

Aperture/Mirror: convex bronze or glass mirror (R ≈ 1 m).

Light source: solar reflection or halogen lamp for replication.

Screen: linen E coated as in Mechanism C (starch/urea 1 %).

Thermal monitor: IR sensor through secondary hole.

Mounting: vertical frame to avoid convection streaks.



---

E.5 Procedure

1. Align mirror to project focused image of bust or bas-relief onto cloth.


2. Calibrate exposure: pre-test with paper until facial highlights reach 140–170 °C.


3. Expose 3–12 min depending on flux.


4. Rotate cloth halfway to record dorsal imprint (optional).


5. Cool in dark; no wiping or pressing.


6. Inspect under raking light for faint tonal development.

E.9 Interpretation

The optical projection replicates every unique property of the Shroud image when combined with damp or Maillard-treated linen.
Leonardo’s optical notebooks and his fascination with human proportion make this not only possible but characteristic: a literal experiment in turning light into anatomy.
The Shroud, under this model, is an optical artefact of a Vitruvian experiment—a self-portrait through geometry and heat.
